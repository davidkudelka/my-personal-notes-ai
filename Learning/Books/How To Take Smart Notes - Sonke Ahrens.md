    * ### Introduction
      * # 1 Everything You Need to Know
        * There is difference between **planners** and **experts**
        * Planners tend to stick to the plans and care about anything else, but experts are always finding new ways how to do things, so simply sticking to a plan is not good enough for them
        * ## 1.1 Good Solutions are Simple - and Unexpected
          * Secret to a successful organisation lies in the holistic perspective
          * Only if you can trust your system, only if you really know that everything will be taken care of, will your brain let go and let you focus on the task at hand
        * ## 1.2 The Slip-box
          * Only if the work is set up in a way that is flexible enough to allow these small and constant adjustments can we keep our interest, motivation and work aligned - which is the precondition to effortless or almost effortless work
          * It is about having the right tools and knowing how to use them - and very few understand that you need both
        * ## 1.3 The slip-box manual
          * You basically collect new notes based on their content, and those notes are linked to another notes with similar content, it is very similar to the Roam research I use right now
      * # 2 Everything You Need to Do
        * Writing is, without dispute, the best facilitator for thinking, reading, learning, understanding and generating ideas we have
        * If you want to improve thinking, the best way how to do it, is to start writing, because writing makes you think about the whole picture
        * ## 2.1 Writing a paper step by step
          * ### Step by step
            * **1. Make fleeting notes** - always mark down your ideas that pops in your mind
            * **2. Make literature notes** - Write down what you read, but be very selective, do not copy sentences, rather form them by your own words
            * **3. Make permanent notes** - Transform those notes from step 1 and step 2 into real notes, where you are using whole sentences and describing text as you would be writing it for someone else
            * **4.Link your notes between each other** - Go through your notes and link them together 
            * **5. Go through your notes** - go through the notes and follow always what is the most interesting for you, think about your notes and generate more ideas from them
            * **6. Select your topics based on your notes** - at this point your notes generated enough ideas to select from
            * **7. Transform notes into coherent text** - don't just copy your notes, turn them into something that make sense together
            * **8. Edit and proofread your manuscript**
          * It happens all the time that you encounter something that you do not need at the moment, but this piece of information might be super useful in the future, and that is exactly why this system is so useful
          * The best ideas are usually the ones we haven't anticipated anyway
      * # 3 Everything You Need to Have

    * ### The Four Underlying Principles
      * # 5 Writing Is the Only Thing That Matters
        * Doing everything as if nothing counts other that writing, improves your learning and listening (all the ways you collect information)
          * Because you focus on the things that actually matter in the conversation, you think more about the questions and everything you consume
      * # 6 Simplicity Is Paramount
        * The slip-box is designed to present you with ideas you have already forgotten, allowing your brain to focus on thinking instead of remembering
        * There are 3 rules you should use when writing to notes and keeping them simple at the same time
          * **Fleeting notes **- simple reminders that goes into the thrash in a day or two
            * They are only useful if you turn them in a day or two into regular notes, if you can’t do this, don’t take them at all
            * They are just reminder of a thought
          * **Permanent notes** - more structured and well-thought notes that should permanently stay in your system
            * They are written in a way that can still be understood even when you have forgotten the context they are taken from
            * Permanent notes can be left without any attention for longer period of time, they are not reminders of something else, but rather complete idea
          * **Project notes** - only relevant to your project, can be discarded or archived after project is finished
            * They can be
              * comments in the manuscript
              * collection of project-related literature
              * outlines
              * snippets or drafts
              * reminders
              * to-do lists
              * and of course the draft itself
        * If you don’t keep this structure, you will soon end up having too many notes which are hard to get through and you will feel like you want to start over
      * # 7 Nobody Ever Starts From Scratch
        * The advice to think about what to write about before you write about it comes too early and too late at the same time
          * **Too late **- it is too late to build up your knowledge of past experiences
          * **Too early** - you should already have some knowledge about the topic 
        * Once you start collecting your notes, you never have to start over again and what’s better you can switch topics, and then come back to them without any need to start all over again
      * # 8 Let the Work Carry You Forward
        * Only if we find our work rewarding in itself, we can get momentum that pushes us forward
        * **Growing mindset** - means getting pleasure from changing for better (it must go from within), NOT getting pleasure from being praised
        * If growth and success are not reasons enough, then maybe the fact that the fear of failure has the ugliest name of all phobias: Kakorrhaphoiphobia xD
        * Making notes from everything we do, shortens feedback loop on our work, we can always share with others our notes from recent books and experiences, we don’t have to wait for your work to be finished
        * Making notes when you read creates a circle, making notes makes you think better about the text, which in return makes you better at finding important parts of the text, which improves writing and writing again improves working with the text
        * Bi-linking works like our brain that’s why it is so useful, slip-box or roam research, we organize our thoughts the same way as we would in our brain, through connections
    * ### The Six Steps to Successful Writing
      * # 9 - Separate and Interlocking Tasks
        * 9.1 Give Each Task Your Undivided Attention
          * We are surrounded by more opportunities that shorten our attention span than opportunities that train our attention span to be longer
          * That is why it is so important to give each task our undivided attention
        * 9.2 Multitasking is not a good idea
          * Multitasking decreases our attention by a big margin, and not only that, we cannot improve in multitasking
          * If you bring more structure to your workflow, you can have more uninterrupted time for focus
        * 9.3 Give Each Task the Right Kind of Attention
          * Writing, reading, proofreading, structuring requires different type of focus and we need to distinguish between them
          * It always helps to first prepare structure and then try to find the right words, after finding right words, we can get back to the structure
          * The best problem solvers were specific in being able to change their type of focus, from hard focus on specific concept to playful exploration of new ideas
        * 9.4 Become an Expert instead of a Planner
          * There is no step-by-step description of what to do at which stage, this comes from experiences
          * You have to get more feedback loops, fail more time to be able to train your mind on when to do which task of the writing
          * Every project is different, that’s why you can’t make plans (Experts don’t make plans)
        * 9.5 Get Closure
          * We have limited capacity of our short-term memory and we tend to remember better the rules and connections rather than facts
          * The best way how to get short-term goals out of our head is to either write them down or complete (that is why Get Things Done work)
          * It is important to write down everything once we are done with thinking, from this we should make as well possible connections (with this at hand we can always come back where we left off)
        * 9.6 Reduce the Number of Decisions
          * We have limited resources to activate willpower, so the best way how to work with it, is to reduce distractions
          * By using the same system with simple set of rules on how to keep your notes and where to write them, reduce number of decisions made by a lot
      * # 10 - Read For Understanding
        * 10.1 Read With a Pen in Hand
          * The idea is not copy but have meaningful dialog with your text
          * By transforming it from text to our own language and notes, we are basically putting those ideas into totally different context (at the beginning the author put it in different context that is our own)
          * When we face something new, our notes tends to be bigger, but this is ok, it is part of progress, more experience we gain, shorter the notes become (sometimes we can summaries whole book with one sentence)
          * Always read with purpose
          * Understanding the meaning of the text is more valuable than making more notes
        * 10.2 Keep an Open Mind
          * We have confirmation bias - we spend time with people we like and we read things we like, which eventually makes us biased towards some facts and avoid others
          * The problem of linear writing is that we are getting more biased, because we chose the topic at start. We should rather just gather information and every counter fact is just interesting progress for us
          * It is good to collect pros and even better to collect cons, because that way we enrich the value of the text
        * 10.3 Get the Gist
          * Finding meaning in text needs practice (specifically practice with our own mind, we have to use our own mind without support of other people to learn it)
          * With practice we can find right words to explain difficult concepts (simple not simplified)
          * The fact that we are able to transform text into our own words prove that we are able to use the information found in the text
        * 10.4 Learn to Read
          * Transforming text into our own words challenge us to understand what we read instead of just being familiar with what we read
          * By choosing a system that forces us to understand text and trash form it to our own text is the best way to avoid mere-exposure effect (being familiar with something)
        * 10.5 Learn by Reading
          * The one that does the hard work is the one who is learning, we know this when we exercise, but in learning it is still revolutionary idea
          * Learning something is mostly about learning how to make connections between all sorts of different information not just learning something as it is independent of the world around us
          * The best learning method is elaboration, because we face our gaps in our arguments and we put effort in retrieving the information
      * # 11 Take Smart Notes
        * It is super important to not just read information as it is given, but to go beyond the text and try to connect the information with other information we have
        * 11.1 Make a Career One Note at a Time
          * You shouldn’t measure your productivity by number of pages written, buy you can do it by number of notes put into your system
        * 11.2 Think Outside the Brain
          * You have to think about the information you read in the context of your whole slip-box, how does it fit there, are there any other notes that can connect to it?
          * By explicitly writing down how something connects or leads to something else, we force ourselves to clarify and distinguish ideas from each other
        * 11.3 Learn by not Trying
          * We have “mental strength”, it is something as mental storage, we can’t really expand it
          * We have as well “mental retrieval” our thoughts are triggered by cues that have connection to those thoughts
            * This is why we want to make as many connections as possible
            * Learning = making meaningful connections
          * Learning is understanding and that’s why best scientist are the best teachers 
          * Writing notes and sorting them into the slip-box is nothing other than an attempt to understand the wider meaning of something
          * Elaborating on the differences and similarities of notes instead of sorting them by topic not only facilitates learning, but facilitates the ability to categorize and create sensible classifications
        * 11.4 Adding Permanent Notes to the Slip-Box
          * Add note with new number and connect it to all other relevant notes
      * # 12 Develop Ideas
        * Every note is just an element in the network or reference and backreferences in the system, from which it gains its quality - Luhmann 
        * 12.1 Develop Topics
          * We have to think about keywords in a way we want to find notes of it. In which circumstances will I want to stumble upon this note, even if I forget about it?
          * Keywords should be always created by the whole context, not by just looking at the note alone
          * This is very important part, because in this phase we are trying to make sense of the note in the whole context of notes
        * 12.2 Make Smart Connections
          * First type is the link between overview note and other relevant notes to this topic, we should always go back and revise this overview note, since it can change as we learn more about the topic
          * Second type is note-to-note, the most typical and can be cross topic
            * These links can find similarities between before unrelated topics
          * Keep in mind that making those connections is the most important thing in our slip-box
        * 12.3 Compare, Correct and Differentiate
          * As working with slip-box we tend to get to one topic multiple times and it force to be confronted with old notes
            * This leads revision of the old ones as we have now more perspectives on the issue or we might have found paradox to our already existing note, this leads to re-thinking of our already collected knowledge and quality of material we are reading
        * 12.4 Assemble a Toolbox for Thinking
          * Information by itself is not that important, what is much more important to always have mental models that actually make sense of this information and connects them together
            * Steps to make connections
              * Pay attention to what you want to remember
              * Properly encode the information you want to keep (this includes thinking about suitable cues)
              * Practice recall
          * By using slip-box we are elaborating on old information to put it into the context, we retrieving old information to connect it to the new contexts, so we are always comparing old with new information
        * 12.5 Use the Slip-Box as a Creativity Machine
          * There is very important process of comparing ideas and spotting little differences in our way of thinking that slip-box offer us
            * This gives us a lot of space for creativity, we can just hang out inside of our notes and compare them with each other
            * This is creativity - not some sudden thought, but small step by step process that helps us to make new connections through comparison
        * 12.6 Think Inside the Box
          * Key to everything is some level of abstraction to our notes, only when we abstract the content away we can put it into the different context, but we still have to keep it specific enough to be able to put it into the practice
            * It is important to have feedback loops to face our errors, successes, mistakes...
            * Always understanding the underlying meaning
            * See what we are actually reading
              * Our brain is making sense of the text and giving it meaning at the same time, so sometimes it is super hard to try to see in text something new
              * We can learn how to be better, but probably we will never be perfect. We can try to ask ourselves **what is not in the picture?**
            * We can be learning much more from focusing on things that doesn't work in order to come up with solutions to the new challenges (we know what doesn't make sense to try)
              * Sometimes redefining the problem is way to go
                * What kind of answer can you expect from asking a question in this particular way? What is missing?
            * **Taking simple ideas seriously** - often simplification of very complicated problem is the biggest breakthrough 
        * 12.7 Facilitate Creativity through Restrictions
          * Even when you can take big notes on a computer, you should focus on taking notes short as possible, best way to do it is to have one note per screen (no scrolling)
          * A clear structure allows us to explore the internal possibilities of something
          * The biggest threat to creativity is **lack of structure**
            * Without structure you can't  compare, differentiate, experiment
          * Slip-box is tool for
            * enforcing distinctions, decisions and making differences visible
      * # 13 Share Your Insight
        * 13.1 From Brainstorming to Slip-Box-Storming
          * When we are using slip-box, we don't have to brainstorm any ideas, ideas rise form the writing we do everyday
          * When we ask question, the quality of this question will prove by time (good question will atract a lot of notes to it, bad question almost none)
        * 13.2 From Top Down to Bottom Up
          * The more familiar we become with ideas we already thought about, the more creative we become, because we have good understanding what we already tried and how it worked
        * 13.3 Getting Things Done by Following Your Interests
          * Everything is about setting up the work so we can change the course whenever we want and we are not getting stuck with our plan.
          * By this we are able to follow what is the most interesting about the topic and what motivates us the most
          * Organising the work so we can steer our projects in the most promising direction not only allows us to stay focused longer, but also to have more fun - and that is a fact 
        * 13.4 Finishing and Review
          * First key point - structure the text and keep it flexible
            * Because of the slip-box we can play with the structure (what is mentioned and when)
          * Another key point - try working on different manuscripts at the same time
            * You can easily switch from one project to another if you feel bored, because you have slip-box
        * 13.5 Becoming an Expert by Giving up Planning
          * Separating our tasks into more manageable and smaller tasks will give us much clearer estimation of what needs to be done 
          * We tend to finish smaller tasks much sooner, because if we can see finish line we tend to speed up
          * Making big plans doesn't make much sense, because our estimations tend to be wrong very often (instead we should plan how many notes we will put into our slip-box - it is easy, small and easily estimated - in course of long time we are able to get much more out of it)
        * 13.6 The Actual Writing
          * You have to convince yourself that first things you are writing down is just first  version that needs to be revised
      * # 14 Make It a Habit
        * Changing the habits is very difficult task
        * The trick is not to try to break with old habits and also not to use willpower to force oneself to do something else, but to strategically build up new habits that have a chance to replace the old ones
