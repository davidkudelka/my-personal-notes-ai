        * Buying your own apartment
          * Timeline: 6 years
          * how much money do you need? 1.500.000

    * ### What's the difference between dividend portfolio vs accumulation portfolio
      * Dividend's tend to be much lower than if you would posses the whole portfolio in accumulation ETF fonds
      * But
        * Every quarter you receive money from dividend's and those dividend's always has to be taxed with 15% tax
      * If
        * You are holding on accumulation ETF fond, it's reinvesting their dividends back to portfolio and when it's the right time to sell you can fulfill time test and do not pay any taxes
    * ### ETF Fonds
      * TER - is percentage on how much the fond management is taking each year, good values should be TER 0,1 -> 0,3, some could be even smaller, but definitely better than 1% or 2% (that's too much)
      * Because of Czech taxes it's the best to pick accumulative ETF fonds, that way 15% of taxes are paid just by the company that owns the assets and they are re-investing this money back to ETF assets
        * If you would pick distribution ETF's, there will be 15% tax to ETF company, which would then send you the money and you would have to pay 15% tax as well (**BAD**)
    * ### How currency affects ETF fonds
      * There few parts the ETF portfolio consists of - your currency, currency of ETF and currency of the assets that are inside of ETF
        * Pick the one that has most of them same
    * ## Diversification
      * ### 2 types of risks
        * Market and Systematic
          * **Market** - the whole market type can go down (stocks, bonds, gold ...)
          * **Systematic** - if you buy just certain segment of stock market (IT segment in stock market, ...)
      * ### Treasury Bonds and Stocks
        * When you are at the beginning of your investing career, it's better to take more risk in terms of owning more stocks
          * -> when approaching later stages, it's better to include more treasury bonds and do rebalancing of your portfolio
      * ### Annualised vs Average returns
        * there is big difference between **Annualised** and **Average** returns. 
          * Average is simply arithmetic number
          * Annualised counts with percentage losses correctly
            * ![[Roam/david_kudelka_notes/Attachments/imgs/app/david_kudelka_notes/m2rTrfDPbg.png]]
        * another interesting number is **standard deviation**
          * that shows how big of a volatility certain assets have 
      * ### Different types of portfolios
        * In the book there are several examples of investment portfolios that you can take look into
          * **What's interesting?**
            * If you are young it's good to pick either full USA stock ETF or Global Stock ETF with **100%** of allocation
              * So either S&P 500, or Vanguard FTSE or iShares Core MSCI World
            * When you are getting older and you don't want to have that big volatility, you should start including more bonds, something like 70-30 or 60-40 portfolios
              * Those portfolios include 70% stock and 30% bonds, or 60% stocks and 40% bonds - advantage of those portfolios is that you usually can have very little number of ETF's included, so rebalancing or just keeping track of it is very easy
          * Look into the book if you want to see more portfolios that are there. Just to mention that most of others are good only if you have significant number of money or you want to retire (so not maximalize profits)
    * ## Where and which ETF fonds to buy? (last chapter)
      * Set of rules you should consider when buying new ETF fond
        * Physical replication > Synthetic replication
        * Lower TER > Higher TER
        * Volume of money maintainer by fond -> Bigger > Smaller
        * Euro > other currencies
        * Accumulative > Distributed
